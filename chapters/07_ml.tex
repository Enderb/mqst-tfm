\section{ML-Assisted State Discrimination}
\label{sec:ml}

\subsection{IQ Data and the Discrimination Problem}

Each single-shot readout produces an averaged $(I, Q)$ pair — a point in the
complex plane.  Repeated preparation and measurement of the qubit in the same
state produces a cloud (cluster) of such points, whose centroid and spread depend
on the readout power, resonator parameters, and integration time.  Two such
clusters, one for $\ket{0}$ and one for $\ket{1}$, must be distinguished
reliably.

For ideal Gaussian clusters of equal covariance, the optimal linear classifier is
the perpendicular bisector of the line segment joining the two centroids:
\begin{equation}
      d(\mathbf{x}) = (\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_0)^\top
      \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}
      \left(\mathbf{x} - \tfrac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_0 + \boldsymbol{\mu}_1)\right)
      \underset{\hat{y}=0}{\overset{\hat{y}=1}{\gtrless}} 0,
      \label{eq:lda}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\mu}_0, \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^2$ are the class
centroids and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is the common covariance matrix (Linear
Discriminant Analysis, LDA~\cite{Gambetta2007}).  When the covariances differ, Quadratic Discriminant
Analysis (QDA) uses the class-specific covariances and yields a curved decision
boundary.

In practice, residual state preparation errors, qubit $T_1$ decay during the
readout pulse, and higher-level leakage ($\ket{2}$, $\ket{3}$) distort the
clusters from ideal Gaussians.  These effects motivate the use of more expressive
classifiers.  The assignment fidelity is defined as
\begin{equation}
      \mathcal{F} = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[P(\hat{s}=0\mid s=0) + P(\hat{s}=1\mid s=1)\bigr],
      \label{eq:fidelity}
\end{equation}
the average per-class accuracy across both prepared states.

\subsection{Lightweight Classifiers for FPGA Deployment}
\label{subsec:classifiers}

For eventual on-FPGA deployment, the classifier must be computable using only
fixed-point arithmetic and a small number of multiply-accumulate operations.
The key constraints are:
\begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Latency}: the decision must complete within one tProcessor cycle
            budget ($\lesssim$\SI{10}{\nano\second}) to avoid extending the readout
            dead time.
      \item \textbf{Resource usage}: the classifier must fit in the available DSP
            slices and block RAM of the FPGA without displacing the readout and
            tProcessor logic.
      \item \textbf{Trainability}: the classifier must be trainable on a laptop from
            a few thousand calibration shots.
\end{itemize}

With these constraints in mind, three classifier families are considered.  A
\textbf{linear threshold} (LDA) reduces to a single dot product and a
comparison — the minimum possible arithmetic, directly implementable in the
tProcessor's \texttt{mathi} instruction.  A \textbf{quadratic threshold} (QDA)
evaluates a $2\times2$ matrix product (4 multiply-accumulates) and handles
asymmetric cluster covariances.  A \textbf{small MLP} with inputs $(I, Q)$, one
or two hidden layers of 4–64 neurons with ReLU activations, and a sigmoid output
can be computed as a matrix-vector product and fits in $\lesssim 200$ 18-bit DSP
slices on the ZCU216.  The following sections report results from evaluating
these classifiers offline on existing experimental data; FPGA deployment is
discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:deployment_path}.

All three classifiers achieve identical fidelity.  The QDA result is
particularly informative: the two IQ clouds do \emph{not} share the same
covariance --- the $\ket{1}$ noise ellipse is approximately 1.7$\times$ wider
than the $\ket{0}$ ellipse (Table~\ref{tab:iq_stats}) --- so QDA's curved
boundary is in principle more expressive than the linear LDA boundary.  That QDA
recovers no additional fidelity is explained by the large cluster separation: the
Mahalanobis distance between the two centroids is approximately 2.4, placing the
clusters well into the regime where the optimal boundary is nearly linear
regardless of covariance asymmetry.  LDA, QDA, and the MLP are all saturating the
classification capacity of the two-dimensional integrated IQ input.

The $\ket{0}$ accuracy (94.0\%) exceeds the $\ket{1}$ accuracy (87.3\%),
pointing at a systematic source of $\ket{1}$ misclassification.  This asymmetry
is the first diagnostic signature of $T_1$ relaxation during the readout window,
which causes some $\ket{1}$ shots to produce an integrated IQ that falls in the
$\ket{0}$ blob.

The result is striking: the fidelity landscape is entirely flat.  No architecture
— across three orders of magnitude in parameter count — improves on LDA.  This is
not a failure of the models; it is a statement about the data.

This result follows directly from the analysis in Part~I.  The two clusters are
well-separated (Mahalanobis distance $\approx 2.4$), placing them in the regime
where the optimal decision boundary is nearly linear even accounting for the
$\approx\!1.7\times$ covariance asymmetry between states --- as confirmed by QDA
matching LDA exactly.  LDA therefore finds a near-Bayes-optimal boundary on this
input, and no classifier --- regardless of expressive power --- can improve on it
using the same two-dimensional integrated IQ features.

\textbf{The implication is fundamental}: further fidelity improvement cannot come
from a more expressive classifier on the same integrated IQ input.  It must come
from richer input features.  The $\ket{1}$ accuracy deficit diagnosed in Part I
identifies the mechanism directly: $T_1$ relaxation mid-shot.

\subsection{Part III — Raw-Trace CNN}
\label{subsec:cnn}

\subsubsection{Motivation and the T1 Relaxation Problem}

As established in Section~\ref{subsec:ml_background}, any classifier operating
on integrated IQ is fundamentally limited when $T_1$ relaxation occurs mid-shot:
the integrator averages over both signal levels and produces a point that falls
between the two clusters, and the information about the true prepared state has
been destroyed.  No classifier operating on this compressed input can recover
these shots, regardless of its expressive power.

The raw ADC time trace, however, encodes the relaxation time $t^*$ directly as a
step discontinuity in the IQ trajectory.  A 1D CNN trained on raw traces can
detect this jump and correctly identify the prepared state even when the
integrated IQ falls between the two clusters.  Part III demonstrates this
capability using physically-grounded synthetic traces, since raw decimated traces
from the ZCU216/QICK setup are not yet available (acquiring them requires using
\texttt{acquire\_decimated} rather than the standard \texttt{acquire} call, and
collecting a dedicated \texttt{SSRO\_raw\_synthetic.h5} dataset --- the simulated
counterpart used here --- or real hardware traces with the board connected to
a qubit chip).  The theoretical motivation for using a CNN rather than a deeper
MLP is discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:ml_background}: convolutional filters are
well-suited to detecting local temporal structure — in this case the step
discontinuity caused by mid-readout relaxation — that pointwise classifiers
cannot exploit.

\subsubsection{Synthetic Trace Model}

Each shot is modelled as a noisy cavity response:
\begin{equation}
      \begin{pmatrix}i(t) \\ q(t)\end{pmatrix}
      = \frac{e(t)}{\bar{e}}\,\boldsymbol{\mu}_{s(t)} + \boldsymbol{\eta}(t),
      \qquad e(t) = 1 - e^{-t/\kappa},
      \label{eq:trace_model}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{e} = \langle e(t)\rangle_t$ normalises the envelope so that
$\langle(i(t), q(t))\rangle_t = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{s(t)}$, matching the real blob
centroids exactly.  The noise $\boldsymbol{\eta}(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},
      T\Sigma_s)$ is chosen so that the sample mean has covariance $\Sigma_s$, matching
the real blob widths.

For $\ket{1}$ shots, qubit relaxation is modelled by drawing a relaxation time
$t^*$ from a geometric distribution with rate $1 - e^{-1/T_1}$.  After $t^*$ the
signal switches from $\boldsymbol{\mu}_1$ to $\boldsymbol{\mu}_0$:
\begin{equation}
      \boldsymbol{\mu}_{s(t)} =
      \begin{cases}
            \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 & t < t^*    \\
            \boldsymbol{\mu}_0 & t \geq t^*
      \end{cases}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{CNN Architecture and Training}

The \texttt{ReadoutCNN} model operates on raw traces of shape $(T, 2)$.  Two
\texttt{Conv1d} layers compress the time axis, global average pooling collapses
it, and a small dense head produces the binary logit for $P(\ket{1})$.  The model
has 953 parameters, sized for deployment via \texttt{hls4ml}.

The CNN achieves perfect accuracy on the 91 relaxation shots in the held-out test
set, compared to 41.8\% for LDA — a gain of 58.2 percentage points on the shots
that are entirely invisible to integration-based methods.  This confirms the
theoretical expectation from Section~\ref{subsec:ml_background}: the CNN is
exploiting the temporal jump structure of the raw trace, not merely interpolating
between blob positions.  The $P(\ket{1})$ confidence as a function of relaxation
time $t^*$ rises monotonically: shots that relax late in the window (spending most
of the trace in $\ket{1}$) are classified with near-certainty, while shots that
relax early (spending most of the trace in $\ket{0}$) are correctly identified as
ambiguous.

These results should be understood as simulation estimates anchored to the real
device data.  The calibration procedure --- adjusting $T_1$ until the synthetic
LDA $\ket{1}$ accuracy matches the measured 87.3\% --- gives $T_1 = 3000$ samples
(${\approx}\SI{15}{\micro\second}$ at \SI{200}{MSps}), corresponding to a
relaxation fraction of approximately 5--6\% of $\ket{1}$ shots within the readout
window (${\approx}91$ relaxation events in a test set of 4000 shots).  The CNN
accuracy of 100\% on relaxation shots should be interpreted with this sample size
in mind; it reflects near-certain classification on the available test shots rather
than a guarantee of perfect generalisation.  The qualitative conclusion is robust:
a CNN operating on raw traces has access to temporal information that the
rectangular integrator discards, and this structural advantage persists regardless
of the exact $T_1$.  Retraining on real raw traces, once collected with
\texttt{acquire\_decimated}, is the natural next step and is discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:conclusions}.

\subsection{FPGA Deployment Path}
\label{subsec:deployment_path}

FPGA deployment of the trained classifiers was not achieved within the scope of
this work.  This section outlines the concrete steps required to close the loop
from the trained models to real-time discrimination inside the QICK firmware.

The current readout pipeline — rectangular integrator followed by the tProcessor
threshold register — already implements LDA implicitly.  Formalising this as an
explicit dot-product instruction (\texttt{mathi}) adds no hardware overhead.  The
CNN requires a separate data path: raw decimated traces from
\texttt{acquire\_decimated} are fed into an \texttt{hls4ml}-synthesised IP block
that produces a binary decision within approximately \SI{100}{\nano\second} to
\SI{200}{\nano\second} at \SI{250}{\mega\hertz}, well within the tProcessor's
feedback budget (Table~\ref{tab:latency}).

The full deployment sequence is:
\begin{enumerate}
      \item \textbf{Collect real raw traces}: connect the ZCU216 to the qubit chip and
            acquire \texttt{SSRO\_raw.h5} using \texttt{acquire\_decimated} (shape
            $(2, N_\text{shots}, T, 2)$).  Set \texttt{readout\_length} to at least
            $3/\kappa$ to capture the full cavity ringdown.  (Note: the file
            \texttt{SSRO\_raw\_synthetic.h5} used in Part~III is a simulated dataset,
            not a hardware capture; this step acquires the real counterpart.)
      \item \textbf{Retrain and validate}: retrain the CNN on real traces, using the
            same calibration approach — adjust $T_1$ in the synthetic model until the
            simulated LDA $\ket{1}$ accuracy matches the measured device value, then
            use the calibrated synthetic dataset to supplement real training data.
      \item \textbf{Export to HLS}: export the trained PyTorch model to ONNX, then
            convert using \texttt{hls4ml} targeting the ZCU216 fabric
            (\texttt{xczu49dr-ffvf1760-2-e}), \texttt{io\_stream}, and fixed-point
            precision \texttt{ap\_fixed<16,6>}.
      \item \textbf{Synthesise and integrate}: synthesise the HLS project in Vivado
            and integrate the resulting IP block into the QICK bitstream alongside the
            existing readout chain.
      \item \textbf{Benchmark}: compare LDA-threshold, CNN-IP, and host-side
            discrimination on the same experimental data to quantify latency reduction
            and fidelity gain under realistic qubit conditions.
\end{enumerate}

Based on the model's 953 parameters and typical \texttt{hls4ml} resource scaling
for comparable architectures, the CNN IP block is expected to require on the order
of \num{10000} LUTs and tens of DSP slices — a small fraction of the ZCU216's
available fabric — though this must be confirmed by synthesis.
